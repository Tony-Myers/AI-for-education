Generative AI for Research

A practical, secure, and methodologically sceptical guide for researchers who want AI to accelerate work without outsourcing judgement.

AI is a collaborator, not evidence

Use models for generation, critique, transformation, and organisation. Scholarly databases, primary sources, code, and expert judgement still carry the evidential weight.

Security and accountability are separate

A secure or local model can protect unpublished work, but it cannot make claims accountable. The researcher remains responsible for every interpretation, citation, and disclosure.

Verification is structural

Hallucination, positional bias, and correlated model errors are not rare accidents. Build checks into prompts, workflows, and publication decisions from the beginning.

Match the model to the task

The question is not whether AI can be inserted into research. It is whether the contribution is defensible, auditable, and proportionate to the evidence.

Voice interview bot

The deck includes a voice-enabled qualitative interview bot. Treat it as an interview support system: test prompts, consent wording, bias, accessibility, and data handling before any live study.

Sandboxed analysis

Use AI to draft deterministic R or Python code, not to calculate complex statistics inside a free-text chat. A sandbox helps contain untrusted code and protects the host system.

Focused notebooks

NotebookLM-style systems are strongest when the source set is curated and narrow. Avoid dumping an entire field into one mega-notebook and treating the summary as synthesis.

Architecture-Driven Prompting

Prompting is context design

Transformer-based systems generate likely continuations from the context window. That makes prompting a methodological act: you are constraining the probability distribution, not asking an oracle.

Temperature

Lower for factual consistency, higher for divergent idea generation.

Top-p

Limits sampling to the most likely cumulative token set.

Top-k

Hard-cuts the candidate token pool to a fixed size.

The 7-part research prompt

Adapted from the workshop and the education AI prompting page.

  1. 1. Role: define the expert lens.
  2. 2. Task: specify the action.
  3. 3. Context: state study design and purpose.
  4. 4. Evidence: supply the data or text to use.
  5. 5. Constraints: forbid unsupported claims.
  6. 6. Format: require tables, code, or headings.
  7. 7. Verification: ask for uncertainty, checks, and missing evidence.
Act as a critical methodological adviser.

Task:
Evaluate the supplied material for factual, statistical, and methodological reliability.

Context:
I am preparing an academic research output and need conservative feedback before submission.

Evidence:
[Paste the source text, table, output, transcript, or model results here.]

Constraints:
Use only the supplied material unless I explicitly ask for external sources. Do not invent citations, missing facts, or statistical values. Distinguish fact, interpretation, and speculation.

Output format:
Use a table with these columns:
Claim | Support status | Evidence | Concern | Required check | Conservative revision.

Verification:
Flag overclaiming, causal language, missing uncertainty, mismatches between evidence and conclusion, and any claim that needs independent database or code verification.

Verification

Fluency is not evidence

The workshop's central risk message is blunt: agreement between polished outputs is not the same thing as independent corroboration. Verification needs its own workflow.

VALID-AI dashboard

Verify sources: confirm that citations exist and say what the model claims.

Assess authority: prioritise peer-reviewed and primary material over plausible grey literature.

Look for bias: inspect omitted methods, geographies, populations, and theoretical positions.

Identify limits: separate supported findings from interpretation and speculation.

Document provenance: record model, prompt, source set, date, and checks performed.

RAG helps, but it is not magic

Retrieval augmented generation moves from closed-book pattern completion to open-book, source-grounded generation. It can reduce hallucination by grounding answers in a trusted corpus, but large undifferentiated notebooks still suffer coverage and context-window problems.

Exploratory RAG: useful for orientation, question generation, and finding candidate passages.
Rigorous synthesis: requires defined inclusion criteria, paper-level extraction, traceable notes, and independent checking.
StatsRAG pattern: build an auditable specification, verify against a trusted local library, then produce a verdict card on compliance, metric integrity, direction, and source support.

Safety protocol for AI-assisted analysis

Do not

Rely on an LLM for direct calculation of complex datasets.

Do

Generate Python or R code and run deterministic statistical procedures.

Do not

Accept test selection without manual verification of the design.

Do

Check normality, variance, outliers, dependence, units, and model assumptions.

Do not

Paste an AI interpretation into a manuscript unchecked.

Do

Constrain the prompt, rerun with variations, compare models, and verify against sources and code.

Secure AI Choices

Choose the smallest exposure that fits the job

The newest secure options are much better than public consumer chat, but "secure" still depends on your institution, licence, data classification, region, retention settings, and whether web grounding or connectors are enabled.

Public web AI

Best for low-risk brainstorming and public information. Do not upload unpublished manuscripts, sensitive participant data, or confidential grant material.

Campus enterprise AI

ChatGPT Edu, Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat, Gemini for Workspace, and Claude for Work offer stronger controls, but local policy decides what data may be used.

Managed secure cloud

Azure AI Foundry, AWS Bedrock, and Google Vertex AI offer tenant-level governance, logging, region choices, and model-provider separation for serious deployments.

Local or self-hosted AI

Ollama, LM Studio, Open WebUI, AnythingLLM, Jan, and GPT4All can run models on your own machine or server. This helps confidentiality, not accuracy.

A simple classification rule

Public data: use any appropriate tool, but verify and cite independently.
Internal or unpublished work: use approved enterprise/campus AI or a managed secure cloud service.
Sensitive, identifiable, or embargoed data: use approved local, self-hosted, or institutionally governed platforms only.

Authorship and Peer Review

Local AI solves confidentiality. It does not solve accountability.

The deck's peer-review section is a useful ethical spectrum: some uses of local AI are highly appropriate, some are risky, and unverified generation is academic misconduct regardless of where the model runs.

UseStatusCore risk
Gap analysis and red teaming Highly appropriate The researcher must decide which critiques are valid.
Grammar, wording, structure Highly appropriate Meaning must remain human-generated and checked.
Substantial drafting Problematic Risks false synthesis, fabricated citations, and authorship blur.
Unverified paste-in Inappropriate The author cannot vouch for accuracy, originality, or source integrity.

Disclosure statement examples

Adapt these to the journal. Always include the tool, version if available, task, date range, and human verification performed.

Copy editing: I used [tool] to suggest grammar, wording, and structure improvements to human-authored text. I reviewed all changes and take responsibility for the final manuscript.

Analysis support: I used [tool] to draft R/Python code and identify possible model assumptions. All analyses were run in [software], checked against the dataset and statistical references, and revised by the authors.

Red teaming: I used [local/approved tool] to identify possible limitations, missing literature, and overclaims. Suggestions were independently evaluated and only incorporated after author review.

Non-negotiable research rules

  • Never cite anything you have not read and verified yourself.
  • Never treat AI output as scholarly evidence.
  • Always verify sources in academic databases.
  • Use AI to assist thinking, not replace it.
  • Write your own argument and own the final judgement.

Future Direction

From co-intelligence to managed agents

The workshop points to a shift from back-and-forth prompting to agentic workflows. The researcher becomes less a prompt typist and more a manager of objectives, constraints, tools, audit logs, and checkpoints.

Agents

Coding and research agents can run multi-step tasks, but require explicit permissions, sandboxing, source boundaries, and review gates.

Fine tuning and LoRA

Low-rank adaptation can specialise models without full retraining. It is powerful, but dataset quality, licensing, evaluation, and drift matter.

Beyond standard transformers

Future systems may reduce context limitations or combine symbolic, retrieval, planning, and model-based reasoning. The verification burden will not disappear.
